Berkshire writings. Apply them to supplied evidence, preserve each card’s limits, and
return unresolved findings when prerequisites are missing.

technology, commodity, or instrument check.

### S01 — Insurance: Value Float by Amount, Cost, Duration, and Outlook

- **Decision question:** Does float create durable financing value or conceal expensive,
  unstable underwriting?
- **Guidance:** Analyze amount, normalized underwriting cost, claim-payment duration,
  runoff, and outlook; compare full cost with realistic funding.
- **Use when:** Material property-casualty operations or investable funds received
  before uncertain claims are paid.
- **Analytical actions:** Reconcile float; estimate cycle-normalized cost and duration;
  stress runoff, reserve deterioration, yield, and cash use.
- **Output:** A float ledger and scenario table with funding comparison, required
  capital, and unresolved reserve effects.
- **Limits:** Float remains a liability and long duration adds reserve uncertainty; an
  equity analogy requires supported low cost and durability.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1986 annual letter** — *defines*: float.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2001 annual letter** — *qualifies*: float value depends on
    relative funding cost.
- **Corroboration:** OM1996; BH2002; BH2014; LOW ch.7 (early float recognition).

<a id="s02"></a>

### S02 — Insurance: Underwrite Exposure and Price Rather than Volume

- **Decision question:** Are risks identifiable and priced for full expected cost, and
  will management shrink when terms are inadequate?
- **Guidance:** Evaluate exposure, conservative severity, full-cost pricing, and
  demonstrated willingness to reject uneconomic volume.
- **Use when:** Insurance underwriting where premium growth may obscure risk selection
  and expected loss.
- **Analytical actions:** Define exposure, limits, and aggregation; compare price with
  loss, expense, capital, and uncertainty; review cohort and volume decisions.
- **Output:** An underwriting-discipline scorecard with exposure, price adequacy,
  expected economics, volume decisions, and unresolved models.
- **Limits:** Shrinking is not always correct and no model removes uncertainty; missing
  exposure or loss assumptions block a conclusion.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1977 annual letter** — *implements*: accept lower volume when
    insurance rates are inadequate.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2010 annual letter** — *implements*: apply four disciplines to
    insurance underwriting.
- **Corroboration:** BH2014; BH2023; BH2025; LOW ch.7 (pricing-over-volume origin).

<a id="s03"></a>

### S03 — Insurance: Reconstruct Reserves and Earnings Through the Cycle

- **Decision question:** How much reported underwriting profit survives accident-year,
  reserve-development, incentive, and cycle scrutiny?
- **Guidance:** Treat earnings as provisional until accident-year development,
  prior-year revisions, settlement maturity, cycles, and quiet years are reconciled.
- **Use when:** Material loss reserves, long-tail claims, catastrophe exposure, changing
  assumptions, or unusually smooth results.
- **Analytical actions:** Build accident- and calendar-year bridges; separate current
  underwriting from reserve changes; normalize cycles.
- **Output:** A reserve-development and normalization table with uncertainty ranges,
  revisions, and unresolved accident years.
- **Limits:** Reserve error may be innocent and even multiyear windows miss rare losses;
  absent triangles leave earnings unresolved.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1984 annual letter** — *supports*: property casualty statements
    are provisional.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1993 annual letter** — *qualifies*: loss free years do not
    establish catastrophe profitability.
- **Corroboration:** BH2002; BH2023; LOW ch.10 (GEICO reserving crisis).

<a id="s04"></a>

### S04 — Insurance: Test Aggregate Tail Loss Against Distress Capacity

- **Decision question:** Can one event or common cause impair solvency, liquidity,
  claims payment, or post-shock capacity?
- **Guidance:** Aggregate policies, businesses, investments, counterparties, collateral,
  and funding; work backward from intolerable outcomes.
- **Use when:** Catastrophe, systemic, liability, cyber, terrorism, or other nonlinear,
  correlated exposure.
- **Analytical actions:** Enumerate common causes; estimate ranges under model breaks;
  compare loss with regulatory, covenant, claims, and post-shock needs.
- **Output:** An aggregate-tail ledger with loss ranges, liquidity timing, binding
  capacity, and unresolved correlations.
- **Limits:** Scenarios are never exhaustive and no universal buffer applies; preserve
  false precision as unresolved.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1996 annual letter** — *implements*: reverse engineer
    aggregated tail risk from intolerable outcomes.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2012 annual letter** — *qualifies*: insurance capacity must
    remain proportionate to enterprise resources.
- **Corroboration:** BH2002; BH2014; LOW ch.23 (super-cat exposure growth).

### S05 — Insurance: Test Crisis Liquidity and the Risk-Transfer Chain

- **Decision question:** Can claims, collateral, refinancing, or counterparties force
  cash shortfalls when the same shock weakens the chain?
- **Guidance:** Analyze claims liquidity and the complete transfer chain together,
  including ability, duration, collateral, jurisdiction, and weakest link.
- **Use when:** Losses are ceded, collateralized, long-tailed, externally financed, or
  dependent on multiple transfer parties.
- **Analytical actions:** Map gross loss, ceded layers, collateral, timing, and parties;
  stress simultaneous claims and failures; identify unrefinanced liquidity.
- **Output:** A risk-transfer and liquidity map showing gross and net timing,
  dependencies, collateral, and unresolved recoverability.
- **Limits:** Retention is not automatically safer and legal rights do not ensure prompt
  collection; unverified recoveries stay unavailable.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2001 annual letter** — *implements*: stress test entire
    reinsurance counterparty chain.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2004 annual letter** — *supports*: reinsurer solvency is
    material counterparty risk.
- **Corroboration:** BH2002; BH2014.

<a id="s06"></a>

### S06 — Insurance: Require Discipline and Structure for a Moat

- **Decision question:** What prevents this apparently commodity-like insurer from
  earning commodity returns?
- **Guidance:** Look for low cost or niche, distribution, underwriting skill, capital,
  and a culture willing to sacrifice volume; do not infer a moat from growth.
- **Use when:** An insurer is claimed to have durable competitive advantage.
- **Analytical actions:** Name and compare the mechanism; test price, retention,
  acquisition cost, losses, distribution, and capital through a cycle; seek persistence.
- **Output:** An insurance-moat map with competitor evidence, cycle indicators, erosion
  signals, and unresolved causal links.
- **Limits:** Berkshire and GEICO are favorable cases, not base rates; unobserved causal
  outcomes leave the moat unresolved.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1987 annual letter** — *supports*: commodity insurance rewards
    low cost or niche.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1999 annual letter** — *qualifies*: reinsurance advantage
    combines distribution skill culture and capital.
- **Corroboration:** BH2014; BH2023; LOW ch.10 (GEICO expense-ratio edge).

### S07 — Banking: Assess Funding, Credit, Leverage, and Culture

- **Decision question:** Can modest asset or underwriting errors overwhelm thin equity
  or trigger confidence-sensitive funding loss?
- **Guidance:** Analyze asset quality, vintages, funding, liquidity, leverage,
  incentives, and bad-news flow as one bank-specific fragility system.
- **Use when:** Banks, deposit-taking lenders, or businesses dependent on
  confidence-sensitive funding.
- **Analytical actions:** Segment credit and concentration; stress funding loss, credit,
  and liquidity together; compare incentives and reporting with outcomes.
- **Output:** A banking resilience matrix covering loss, runoff, liquidity, capital,
  culture indicators, and unresolved disclosures.
- **Limits:** Regimes and protections differ materially; missing asset or funding detail
  blocks the branch.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2006 annual letter** — *illustrates*: teaser mortgages defer
    and amplify payment stress.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2010 annual letter** — *supports*: leverage can destroy
    survival when refinancing disappears.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.G; OM1996; BH2002; LOW ch.19 (deposit-insurance moral
  hazard).

### S08 — Consumer and Retail: Separate Brand Economics from Fragile Distribution

- **Decision question:** Do loyalty, pricing, distribution, inventory, and format cause
  durable returns, or does the brand depend on a weakening channel?
- **Guidance:** Test customer behavior and price separately from distribution access,
  delivered value, inventory risk, and format durability.
- **Use when:** Consumer products, retail, restaurants, or similar brand and channel
  dependence.
- **Analytical actions:** Measure price, volume, retention, mix, and channel economics;
  trace inventory and format risks; identify channel shifts and alternatives.
- **Output:** A franchise and distribution scorecard with behavior, unit economics,
  dependencies, and erosion indicators.
- **Limits:** Exceptional management can overcome some weak economics; missing cohort,
  channel, or inventory evidence leaves durability unresolved.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1983 annual letter** — *supports*: consumer franchise and low
    cost position create goodwill.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1988 annual letter** — *qualifies*: exceptional management can
    overcome mediocre industry economics.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.E; EWB §V.C; LOW ch.18 (Coca-Cola franchise case).

### S09 — Infrastructure: Evaluate Regulation, Irreplaceability, and Maintenance Capital

- **Decision question:** Do essential demand and network position earn adequate owner
  returns after obligations, regulation, maintenance, and financing stress?
- **Guidance:** Evaluate regulated return, irreplaceability, service duties, required
  capital, political legitimacy, and stressed financing together.
- **Use when:** Utilities, railways, pipelines, networks, and regulated or essential
  capital-intensive infrastructure.
- **Analytical actions:** Reconcile allowed and realized returns; separate maintenance,
  replacement, and expansion; stress lag, affordability, utilization, and debt service.
- **Output:** An infrastructure bridge covering rate mechanism, capital, returns,
  financing, constraints, and unresolved regulation.
- **Limits:** Irreplaceability does not guarantee owner returns; missing rate or
  maintenance evidence blocks the judgment.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2015 annual letter** — *implements*: regulated investment
    requires reasonable returns and constituency approval.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2003 annual letter** — *qualifies*: stable ring fenced utility
    cash flows can support subsidiary debt.
- **Corroboration:** OM1996; BH2014.

### S10 — Technology and Media: Test Whether Disruption Strengthens or Destroys the Moat

- **Decision question:** Can important economics be forecast despite obsolescence,
  distribution change, network effects, and shifting attention?
- **Guidance:** Identify the protection mechanism, then test technical substitution,
  platform control, distribution, behavior, and reinvestment; stop if forecastability
  cannot be bounded.
- **Use when:** Technology, software, platforms, media, advertising, or rapid technical
  and distribution change.
- **Analytical actions:** Map mechanism and dependencies; track retention, switching,
  network density, distribution, attention, and innovation spend; define exit
  indicators.
- **Output:** A disruption and forecastability map with dependencies, erosion
  indicators, and an estimable or outside-boundary result.
- **Limits:** Leadership, reputation, or network size alone is insufficient; unbounded
  critical economics stop the branch.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1993 annual letter** — *qualifies*: stay with easy to
    understand businesses.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2005 annual letter** — *defines*: widening the moat.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.E; BH2014; LOW ch.18 (competence-bounded selection).

<a id="s11"></a>

### S11 — Energy and Commodities: Normalize Cycles, Inflation, and Capital Intensity

- **Decision question:** Do normalized owner returns arise from durable advantage or
  favorable price, cycle, or underinvestment?
- **Guidance:** Separate price exposure from controllable advantage and normalize
  volume, price, utilization, depletion, capital, regulation, and financing.
- **Use when:** Producers, processors, commodity-linked assets, or energy businesses.
- **Analytical actions:** Build price-volume-cost and cycle bridges; estimate
  maintenance, depletion, and working capital; test cost claims under adverse prices and
  capacity.
- **Output:** A normalized commodity table with cycle range, capital burden, cost
  position, inflation exposure, and unresolved assumptions.
- **Limits:** Low-cost producers may be exceptions and price forecasts remain uncertain;
  missing reserves or replacement data blocks owner cash.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1978 annual letter** — *supports*: commodity capital intensity
    depresses returns.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1985 annual letter** — *qualifies*: commodity cost saving capex
    becomes industry price reduction.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §I.C; EWB §V.C; OM1996; BH2014.

### S12 — Special Instruments: Analyze Event Probability, Duration, Downside, and Optionality

- **Decision question:** Are event, duration, cash-flow, downside, and optionality
  mechanics understandable and adequately evidenced?
- **Guidance:** Route each instrument to atomic contractual cash-flow,
  event-probability, duration, upside, failure-downside, and enforceability analysis;
  frozen evidence supports only announced-event arbitrage.
- **Use when:** Announced-event arbitrage, workout, preferred, convertible, or unusual
  contractual claim rather than ordinary equity.
- **Analytical actions:** Identify contract, event, states, and legal dependencies;
  estimate probability, duration, terms, and failure loss; stop unsupported branches.
- **Output:** A state-contingent worksheet with event states, probability inputs,
  duration, payoff and loss ranges, legal dependencies, and unsupported branches.
- **Limits:** No universal return, probability, diversification, or legal checklist
  applies; unsupported instruments remain unresolved.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1988 annual letter** — *defines*: risk arbitrage.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1988 annual letter** — *implements*: four question arbitrage
    analysis.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.B; BPL1964.
