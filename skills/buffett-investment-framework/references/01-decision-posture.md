# Decision Posture and Opportunity Cost

Use these cards as evidence-bounded analytical prompts derived from Buffett and
Berkshire writings. Apply them to supplied evidence, preserve each card’s limits, and
return unresolved findings when prerequisites are missing.

Read this file when framing competence, ownership, alternatives, patience,
concentration, active skill, or speculation.
Load only the cards selected by the router; do not load the whole module by default.
Abbreviated corroboration citations resolve in the [source key](00-source-key.md).

## Complete Cards

<a id="d01"></a>

### D01 — Establish the Circle of Competence as a Forecastability Boundary

- **Decision question:** Can the critical economics be bounded over the relevant horizon
  without unsupported market or macro forecasts?
- **Guidance:** Continue only when the few variables governing competitive economics and
  owner cash can be identified and bounded; otherwise return an outside-competence or
  unresolved finding.
- **Use when:** Initial screening, an unfamiliar business, or analysis dependent on
  technological, regulatory, commodity, or macro forecasts.
- **Analytical actions:** Name the critical variables and horizon; bound supported base
  and downside cases; identify changes that invalidate the bounds.
- **Output:** A competence-boundary note classified inside, conditional, outside, or
  unresolved, with decisive variables and missing evidence.
- **Limits:** Understandability does not establish quality, value, or low risk; do not
  invent precision for an unbounded variable.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Warren Buffett’s 2015 50th-anniversary essay** — *implements*: limit investments
    to evaluable prospects.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2004 annual letter** — *implements*: prefer simple propositions
    with few critical variables.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.E; BPL1967; BPL1970.

<a id="d02"></a>

### D02 — Analyze Equities as Fractional Ownership of an Explainable Business

- **Decision question:** Would the proportional business interest remain analyzable if
  no quoted price were available?
- **Guidance:** Analyze common stock as a claim on business economics, management, cash
  generation, obligations, and value while preserving differences between minority and
  control ownership.
- **Use when:** Security analysis in which price action, ticker metrics, or trading
  narratives risk replacing business analysis.
- **Analytical actions:** Describe the economic claim and rights; explain value drivers
  without the quote; identify control, tax, liquidity, and minority-rights differences.
- **Output:** A fractional-ownership synopsis covering the owner claim, value drivers,
  impairments, and control limitations.
- **Limits:** Fractional ownership can differ materially from control; the posture
  establishes neither an attractive price nor an action.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Warren Buffett’s 1984 essay “The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville”** —
    *defines*: always buying the business not the stock.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1980 annual letter** — *qualifies*: minority ownership can be
    better than control.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.A; OM1996; BH2014.

<a id="d03"></a>

### D03 — Invert the Thesis and Seek Disconfirming Evidence

- **Decision question:** What evidence, mechanism, or outcome would make the preferred
  thesis wrong?
- **Guidance:** Convert decisive claims into falsifiable propositions, develop the
  strongest contrary account, and predeclare evidence that would weaken, revise, or
  reject each claim.
- **Use when:** Before accepting a thesis or memo conclusion, and after material new
  evidence.
- **Analytical actions:** Restate claims so they can fail; search for the strongest
  contrary explanation; record invalidation conditions and missing evidence.
- **Output:** A rebuttal ledger linking each claim to counterevidence, alternate
  mechanisms, and an unresolved or invalidation condition.
- **Limits:** Inversion can become performative skepticism or hindsight; absence of
  disconfirmation is not confirmation.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1985 annual letter** — *implements*: study mistakes to avoid
    repetition.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2019 annual letter** — *specializes*: boards should hear
    informed opposition to acquisitions.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.F; CM50-2015; BH2014.

<a id="d04"></a>

### D04 — Separate Business Value from Market Price

- **Decision question:** Is the conclusion responding to changed business evidence or
  merely to a changed quotation?
- **Guidance:** Treat the quote as an optional offer and information input, not a
  substitute for independently assessed economics and value.
- **Use when:** Price volatility, relative-performance pressure, or an argument whose
  principal support is a recent price move.
- **Analytical actions:** Update operating evidence and value; test whether the move
  conveys new fundamental information; compare price with the supported value range.
- **Output:** A price-versus-value bridge distinguishing fundamental change, possible
  information content, sentiment, and unresolved causes.
- **Limits:** Prices can reveal information and value estimates can be wrong; no
  convergence timing or action follows automatically.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1987 annual letter** — *defines*: Mr.
    Market.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2013 annual letter** — *implements*: ignore daily prices and
    monitor productive output.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.C; OM1996; BH2022.

<a id="d05"></a>

### D05 — Use Opportunity Cost Across Action and Inaction

- **Decision question:** Which feasible alternative—the current asset, cash, another
  asset, or waiting—has the best supported owner outcome after tax, risk, and friction?
- **Guidance:** Compare action and inaction on a common owner-level basis and treat
  waiting as an alternative with optionality and opportunity cost.
- **Use when:** Allocation comparisons or a proposal that omits the best feasible
  alternative.
- **Analytical actions:** Define alternatives and horizon; compare owner cash, risk,
  tax, friction, liquidity, and scale; state assumptions driving the ranking.
- **Output:** An opportunity-cost table with supported, challenged, or unresolved
  comparisons.
- **Limits:** Alternatives may be incommensurable or poorly evidenced; the comparison
  does not authorize reallocation.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1987 annual letter** — *implements*: select by after tax
    mathematical expectation.
  - **Warren Buffett’s 2015 50th-anniversary essay** — *qualifies*: consider inaction
    before selling a prosperous business.
- **Corroboration:** BPL1963; CM50-2015; BH2014.

<a id="d06"></a>

### D06 — Pair Patient Standards with Decisive Action

- **Decision question:** Are standards being lowered to create activity, or is a
  supported opportunity being missed because of pressure or hesitation?
- **Guidance:** Precommit analytical and resilience requirements, wait without relaxing
  them, and classify a case as analytically ready only when evidence, price, and
  downside align.
- **Use when:** Activity pressure, fear or enthusiasm, prolonged inactivity, or a
  material price dislocation.
- **Analytical actions:** Record precommitted conditions; test the case against them;
  explain a ready, not-ready, or unresolved result.
- **Output:** An analytical-readiness checklist and reasoned no-action, unresolved, or
  criteria-satisfied finding.
- **Limits:** Patience is not proof of discipline and contrarian action is not
  automatically correct; the card does not execute an action.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2024 annual letter** — *supports*: wait liquidly and act on
    compelling opportunities.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2010 annual letter** — *qualifies*: persistent uncertainty
    should not paralyze long term investment.
- **Corroboration:** CM50-2015; BH2014; BH2022.

<a id="d07"></a>

### D07 — Assess Whether Concentration Fits Capability and Portfolio Survival

- **Decision question:** Is concentration supported by understanding, distinct
  exposures, downside tolerance, liquidity, and survival of a major error?
- **Guidance:** Evaluate concentration only where conviction is evidence-based and
  survival does not depend on the thesis being right.
- **Use when:** A concentrated exposure, correlated portfolio, or claim that conviction
  alone justifies concentration.
- **Analytical actions:** Map competence and downside; aggregate common exposures and
  forced-action paths; identify a major-error survival case.
- **Output:** A concentration-fit assessment with capability, correlation, liquidity,
  and survival findings, but no position size.
- **Limits:** Self-assessed competence may be false; no numeric sizing rule is supplied.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1984 annual letter** — *qualifies*: concentration requires
    exceptional financial strength.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1993 annual letter** — *implements*: match diversification to
    investor knowledge.
- **Corroboration:** BPL1963; OM1996; LOW ch.5 (American Express concentration case).

<a id="d08"></a>

### D08 — Judge Active Skill After Friction, Capacity, and Precommitment

- **Decision question:** Was apparent skill identifiable prospectively, repeatable after
  costs, and usable at the capital scale under review?
- **Guidance:** Compare with a suitable low-friction alternative and reject
  hindsight-selected success unless process, persistence, downside behavior, incentives,
  and capacity support a prospective claim.
- **Use when:** Manager selection, strategy evaluation, performance attribution, or
  extrapolation from a small capital base.
- **Analytical actions:** Reconstruct after-cost results; examine prospective selection
  evidence and downside periods; test capacity and incentive effects.
- **Output:** A skill dossier separating gross result, friction, exposure, capacity, and
  prospective identifiability.
- **Limits:** Benchmark, factor, regime, survivorship, and access choices can leave the
  conclusion unresolved.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2016 annual letter** — *qualifies*: skilled managers exist but
    are rarely identifiable.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2016 annual letter** — *supports*: asset growth turns
    investment success into underperformance.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §III.A; BPL1969; BH2022.

<a id="d09"></a>

### D09 — Detect Reflexive Speculation and Promotional Supply

- **Decision question:** Is expected value grounded in productive cash generation or in
  leverage, promotion, serial supply, and resale to later buyers?
- **Guidance:** Treat fee-driven distribution, serial issuance, effortless-gain
  narratives, and exit-before-others logic as warning mechanisms that require
  independently supported business value.
- **Use when:** New-issue booms, promotional markets, nonproductive assets, rapid
  issuance, or a thesis dominated by resale.
- **Analytical actions:** Trace cash generation and who funds whom; map issuance,
  leverage, incentives, and supply; separate productive return from resale.
- **Output:** A speculation-mechanism map identifying productive support, reflexive
  dependencies, beneficiaries, and unresolved economics.
- **Limits:** The warning neither times reversal nor implies that every high valuation,
  intermediary, or issuance is fraudulent.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1990 annual letter** — *supports*: distribution revenue can
    overpower security merit.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2000 annual letter** — *supports*: bubble markets enable
    investor extraction business models.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.G; BPL1965; BH2023.
