# Business Economics and Competitive Position

Use these cards as evidence-bounded analytical prompts derived from Buffett and
Berkshire writings. Apply them to supplied evidence, preserve each card’s limits, and
return unresolved findings when prerequisites are missing.

Read this file when mapping the business, testing moat mechanisms, reinvestment, or
structural change. Load only the cards selected by the router; do not load the whole
module by default. Abbreviated corroboration citations resolve in the
[source key](00-source-key.md).

## Complete Cards

<a id="b01"></a>

### B01 — Map How the Business Earns and Converts a Dollar

- **Decision question:** How does a customer dollar become revenue, operating cash,
  required reinvestment, and distributable owner cash?
- **Guidance:** Build a causal model from transaction and unit economics through working
  capital, maintenance, growth funding, and owner cash, then reconcile it to reported
  totals.
- **Use when:** Initial analysis, a changed business model, unexplained cash conversion,
  or a forecast with unclear operating mechanics.
- **Analytical actions:** Map revenue drivers, unit economics, and costs; trace required
  and optional reinvestment; reconcile to reported cash and segments.
- **Output:** A one-page business-model map and dollar-conversion bridge with explicit
  gaps.
- **Limits:** A coherent map does not prove durability or value; unresolved segment,
  unit, or reinvestment data leave the map unresolved.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2000 annual letter** — *supports*: growth affects value only
    through cash flow economics.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1977 annual letter** — *illustrates*: See’s capital light
    earnings growth.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §V.C; EWB §V.D; OM1996.

<a id="b02"></a>

### B02 — Identify a Causal Moat and Track Its Direction

- **Decision question:** What mechanism protects excess returns, and is it widening or
  eroding?
- **Guidance:** Name the mechanism, behavior it changes, and economics it protects;
  track direction with business-specific evidence rather than labels.
- **Use when:** A thesis claims durable quality, excess returns, franchise value, or
  competitive advantage.
- **Analytical actions:** State the mechanism and protected benefit; describe the
  strongest plausible attack; track strengthening and erosion indicators.
- **Output:** A causal moat map with mechanism, protected economics, attack path,
  indicators, and unresolved links.
- **Limits:** Reputation, market share, and historical returns do not alone prove a
  moat.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2005 annual letter** — *defines*: widening the moat.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1995 annual letter** — *illustrates*: GEICO direct distribution
    created structural cost advantage.
- **Corroboration:** BH1981; BH2007; BH2014; LOW ch.3 (GEICO cost-advantage discovery).

<a id="b03"></a>

### B03 — Test Pricing Power, Brand, and Distribution Strength

- **Decision question:** Do customer behavior, channel position, and realized pricing
  support durable economics?
- **Guidance:** Treat brand and distribution as mechanisms only when price realization,
  retention, volume, utility, and channel evidence show a durable behavioral effect.
- **Use when:** Brand, loyalty, distribution, or pricing power is central to the thesis.
- **Analytical actions:** Decompose price, volume, mix, retention, and elasticity;
  examine channels and utility; test inflation, scarcity, and promotion as alternatives.
- **Output:** A pricing-and-distribution scorecard with mechanism evidence,
  alternatives, and direction.
- **Limits:** Price increases can reflect inflation or scarcity; awareness and past
  margins are insufficient.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1983 annual letter** — *supports*: consumer franchise and low
    cost position create goodwill.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1993 annual letter** — *supports*: brands products and
    distribution create protective moats.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §V.C; BH2014; BH2023; LOW ch.9 (See’s brand pricing case).

<a id="b04"></a>

### B04 — Test Cost, Scale, Switching, Network, and Efficient-Scale Advantages

- **Decision question:** Does a non-brand mechanism durably alter delivered cost,
  customer switching, competitor entry, or network utility?
- **Guidance:** Verify each mechanism through unit-cost, customer, network, or entry
  evidence; do not infer durability from size alone.
- **Use when:** A thesis rests on low cost, scale, switching costs, network effects,
  scarce capacity, or efficient scale.
- **Analytical actions:** Quantify the claimed difference; test whether rivals can copy
  or bypass it; track switching, network density, entry, and delivered cost.
- **Output:** A mechanism-specific advantage table with causal link, rival response,
  durability, and unresolved evidence.
- **Limits:** Scale can add complexity, switching can be inertia, networks can reverse,
  and temporary efficiency is not a moat.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1995 annual letter** — *illustrates*: GEICO direct distribution
    created structural cost advantage.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2007 annual letter** — *defines*: enduring economic moat.
- **Corroboration:** CM50-2015; BH1981; BH2014.

<a id="b05"></a>

### B05 — Distinguish a Franchise from a Commodity or Capital Trap

- **Decision question:** Will price-taking, excess capacity, and required reinvestment
  consume apparent earnings or growth?
- **Guidance:** Diagnose whether returns come from differentiated value or favorable
  cycle and price while replacement and competitive capital absorb owner cash.
- **Use when:** Commodity, manufacturing, transport, capital-intensive, or rapidly
  growing businesses with volatile prices or persistent reinvestment.
- **Analytical actions:** Measure replacement capital, working capital, utilization, and
  incremental returns; test price-taking and capacity; identify low-cost or regulated
  exceptions.
- **Output:** A franchise-versus-capital-trap diagnosis with capital burden, industry
  mechanism, exceptions, and unresolved data.
- **Limits:** A low-cost producer or fairly regulated asset can be viable; one weak
  cycle does not establish a trap.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1978 annual letter** — *supports*: commodity capital intensity
    depresses returns.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1982 annual letter** — *supports*: commodity overcapacity
    drives poor profitability.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §I.C; OM1996; BH1981; BH2023.

<a id="b06"></a>

### B06 — Judge the Reinvestment Runway from Normalized Capital Returns

- **Decision question:** Can the business deploy material additional capital at
  attractive normalized returns for long enough to affect owner value?
- **Guidance:** Separate returns on required from incremental capital, then test the
  scale, duration, funding, and durability of the prospective runway.
- **Use when:** Growth valuation, retained-earnings analysis, or a quality claim based
  on historical returns.
- **Analytical actions:** Measure normalized unleveraged returns; estimate deployable
  capital and funding; stress return decay with scale and competition.
- **Output:** A reinvestment-runway schedule showing return evidence, deployable
  capital, duration, decay assumptions, and unresolved bounds.
- **Limits:** Historical returns do not prove future opportunity; neither reinvestment
  nor distribution alone establishes security value.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1992 annual letter** — *supports*: best and worst businesses by
    incremental capital returns.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 2007 annual letter** — *qualifies*: scale prevents repetition
    of Berkshire past returns.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §III.C; OM1996; BH2014; BH2023.

<a id="b07"></a>

### B07 — Separate Cyclical Setbacks from Structural or Turnaround Traps

- **Decision question:** Is weakness temporary and bounded, or caused by persistent
  economics that additional effort and capital are unlikely to repair?
- **Guidance:** Identify the causal problem and require evidence that it is discrete,
  financeable, and repairable before calling it cyclical or a turnaround.
- **Use when:** Depressed earnings, apparent cheapness, restructuring, turnaround
  claims, or repeated capital demands.
- **Analytical actions:** Separate cycle, execution, and structural mechanisms; compare
  prior cycles and peers; define repair conditions, capital, and failure indicators.
- **Output:** A cyclical-versus-structural diagnosis with causal evidence, repair test,
  capital requirement, and unresolved classification.
- **Limits:** Poor historical economics are not immutable and exceptional management can
  improve a mediocre business.
- **Source basis:**
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1979 annual letter** — *illustrates*: Waumbec cheap turnaround
    failed.
  - **Berkshire Hathaway 1988 annual letter** — *qualifies*: exceptional management can
    overcome mediocre industry economics.
- **Corroboration:** EWB §II.F; OM1996; BH2014; FL2025.
